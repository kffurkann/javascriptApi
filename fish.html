Fish

Fish is the general name of cold-blooded vertebrates with different evolutionary lines that live in fresh and salt water. This term describes a way of life rather than a classification. Fish living today are generally grouped under 5 classes. These classes are as different from each other as the 4 classes of air-breathing animals: amphibians, reptiles, birds and mammals.

Fish, which have a history of approximately 450 million years, have evolved to adapt to almost all types of aquatic environments during this time. Since they underwent a great change during the transition to the land environment and turned into 4-legged land vertebrates, these aquatic creatures are actually the first ancestors of land vertebrates.

When we say fish, we usually think of an aquatic animal that has fins, gills, a respiring body, and a slippery body suitable for moving in water. However, the number of fish that do not fit this description is much higher than those that do. The bodies of some of them are expanded longitudinally, some of them are short, flattened especially in those living at the bottom, and in many of them they are pressed from the sides. The position of their mouths, eyes, nostrils and gills also varies greatly from species to species.

The basic structure and function of the fish body is similar to that of all other vertebrates. The four basic tissues that make up the body of land vertebrates are also present in fish: epithelial tissue covering the outer surfaces, connective and support tissue (bone, cartilage and fibrous tissues and their derivatives), nervous tissue and muscle tissue. The typical fish body is spindle-shaped with a streamlined profile adapted for swimming: it consists of a head, body and tail.

The trunk cavity, which contains vital organs, is usually on the lower front side of the body. At the posterior end of this cavity, just in front of the base of the anal fin, is the anal hole through which feces are discharged. The spinal cord and vertebrae start from the posterior part of the skull skeleton, pass through the dorsal body cavity and tail area, and end at the base of the caudal fin.

Although there are many different types of reproduction in fish, the most common is based on the fertilization of countless small eggs laid by the female in the water outside the body. The eggs of surface fish in the open sea are generally clear as if suspended in water; Coastal and freshwater fish lay their eggs on the seabed or among plants; Some species even stick their eggs to rocks or plants with a secretion. The sperm that will fertilize the eggs are produced in the 2 (sometimes 1) testes in the trunk cavity of the male and are discharged into the water with a liquid of milky consistency and color. In bony fish, a sperm duct originating from each of the testicles opens into the urogenital hole behind the anus, and in sharks and rays, it opens into the feces. Some fish also have a type of mating organ that allows the male to ejaculate his sperm into the female's oviduct (internal fertilization).

If we look at fish in terms of sensory organs; Their sense of smell is of great importance to almost all fish. Some eels with very small eyes rely on their sense of smell rather than sight to locate their food.

The sense of taste is also very developed in most fish; There are taste organs not only in the oral cavity but also in some parts of the head and body. Perhaps the most important organ in terms of nutrition, avoiding dangers, and reproduction and survival is the eye. Although the eyes of fish are similar to those of all other vertebrates in terms of their basic structure and function, they show different characteristics because they are adapted to very different living conditions. Fish that live in dark and dim environments generally have large eyes. However, if another sense develops excessively and becomes dominant, the function of the eyes decreases. For them, sound perception and balance are two senses that are closely related to each other. Sound waves, especially low-frequency waves, that spread easily in the water, hit the head and body fluids and bones of the fish and are transmitted to the hearing organs. The range of sound frequencies detectable by fish is very different from that of humans; This comes from the speed of sound propagation in water. It is thought that many fish communicate with each other by grinding their teeth against each other or making sounds in other ways.
